## What to Know Before You Buy

Before we get into specific picks, let's talk about what actually matters when you're choosing a trolling motor battery. There's a lot of spec-sheet noise out there.

**Amp-hours (Ah) is your runtime budget.** A 100Ah battery theoretically gives you 100 amps over one hour, or more practically, about 5 amps for 20 hours. Your trolling motor's actual draw depends heavily on speed setting. A 55 lb thrust motor pulling 5 amps at low speed versus 40+ amps at full throttle means your real-world runtime varies wildly. Most anglers operating at medium speed can expect a 100Ah battery to last a full 8-hour day.

**Usable capacity is where lithium wins.** A lead-acid battery (AGM or flooded) should only be discharged to 50% capacity to preserve battery life. That 100Ah AGM is really 50Ah of usable juice. A lithium (LiFePO4) battery can safely discharge to 80-90% — so that 100Ah lithium gives you 80-90Ah of real use. This is the core math that justifies the price premium for serious anglers.

**Weight matters more than people admit.** If you're fishing a kayak or a small jonboat, adding a 68-lb battery is a meaningful tradeoff. Lithium batteries weigh 50-60% less than comparable lead-acid options.

**AGM vs. flooded lead-acid.** Flooded batteries are cheap and rechargeable but require maintenance (checking water levels, careful ventilation) and can't be mounted on their side. AGM (Absorbed Glass Mat) batteries are sealed, maintenance-free, and can be mounted in any orientation. For most anglers, AGM is worth the modest price premium over flooded.

**Charger compatibility.** Lithium batteries require a lithium-compatible charger. If you're switching from AGM to lithium, budget for a new charger. Using a standard lead-acid charger on a lithium battery will damage it and potentially create a safety hazard.

## Trolling Motor Battery FAQ

**Q: How do I know what size battery I need for my trolling motor?**

Match battery capacity to your motor's thrust rating and your typical fishing duration. As a rough rule: 55 lb thrust motors typically draw 40–52 amps at full throttle, 5–10 amps at low. For an all-day trip (8 hours at moderate use), a 100Ah deep-cycle battery is the minimum I'd recommend. For 24V or 36V systems running higher-thrust motors (80–112 lbs), you'll need two or three batteries in series. Don't try to run a 112 lb thrust Minn Kota on a single 12V 100Ah battery — you'll destroy it and yourself.

**Q: Can I use a car battery for my trolling motor?**

Technically yes. Practically, don't. Car batteries are designed for high-current starting bursts followed by immediate recharge from the alternator. Deep cycling them — which is exactly what a trolling motor does — destroys them rapidly. You'll likely kill a car battery in a single season. Spend the extra money on a proper deep-cycle marine battery.

**Q: How long does it take to charge a trolling motor battery?**

A fully discharged 100Ah battery with a 10-amp charger takes roughly 10–12 hours to reach full charge. With a 15-amp charger, you're looking at 7–8 hours. Smart chargers with bulk/absorption/float stages are worth every penny — they charge faster and extend battery life by preventing overcharging. Never leave a non-smart charger connected indefinitely.
