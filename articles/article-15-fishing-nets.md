## Why Your Net Choice Actually Matters

Before the reviews, here's the framework. Two decisions define every net purchase:

**Rubber vs. Nylon Mesh**

Nylon mesh — especially knotted nylon — is the enemy of fish slime, scales, and survival rates. The knots scrape protective mucus off fish you plan to release. If you practice catch-and-release even occasionally, rubber or knotless rubber-coated mesh is non-negotiable. Rubber mesh is also harder for hooks to tangle in, which means you spend less time on the bank cursing and more time fishing.

Knotless nylon is acceptable for fish you're keeping. It's cheaper, lighter, and dries faster. On a kayak where you're putting fish in a cooler, knotless nylon is fine. On a trout stream where everything goes back, use rubber.

**Floating vs. Non-Floating**

Drop a non-floating net from a kayak or a dock and it sinks. That's a simple physics problem with an expensive solution. Foam-core handles or foam-injected hoops solve it. If you fish from a kayak, canoe, or dock more than twice a year, pay the premium for a floating net. If you wade fish with the net clipped to your vest, it's irrelevant.

## FAQ

**What size net do I need for bass fishing?**
For largemouth bass, minimum hoop size is 18x20 inches. Most fish you'll encounter fit in that range, but a 20x23-inch hoop like the Frabill gives you margin for bigger fish without adding much bulk. Go smaller only if you're targeting smallmouth in streams where pack-ability matters more.

**Is rubber mesh really worth the extra cost over nylon?**
Yes, for any fish you're releasing. Nylon mesh — especially knotted nylon — strips the protective slime coat off fish, which leads to infection and mortality after release. Rubber or rubber-coated knotless mesh is significantly gentler. The price difference is usually $10–$20. It's not negotiable if catch-and-release is part of your practice.

**Do I need a floating net for kayak fishing?**
Strongly recommended. A non-floating net dropped overboard in 12 feet of water is gone. Foam-core handles add minimal cost and weight. If you're kayak fishing more than occasionally, spend the extra money on a floating net. The Plusinno is the best budget option for that use case.

**How long should a fishing net handle be?**
It depends on your fishing position. Wade fishers and fly anglers can work with 14–24 inch handles — longer handles snag brush. Bank anglers and boat fishers benefit from 36–48 inch handles to reach fish without leaning dangerously far over the water. Telescoping handles solve the problem if you fish multiple environments.
