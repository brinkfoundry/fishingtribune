## What to Look For in a Fish Grip Tool

Before we get into specific picks, here's what separates a grip you'll rely on from one that sits in your tackle bag untouched:

**Jaw mechanism reliability.** The trigger should lock cleanly with one hand. You're often holding a rod in the other hand while the fish is still green. Anything that requires two hands to operate or has a loose, sloppy jaw lock is a liability.

**Material and corrosion resistance.** Freshwater anglers can often get away with quality aluminum or ABS plastic. Saltwater anglers need stainless steel or marine-grade polymer — anything else will pit and seize within a few seasons.

**Integrated scale.** Not essential, but a reliable built-in scale is a genuine convenience for catch-and-release anglers who want weight data without fumbling for a separate tool.

**Floating design.** If you're fishing from a kayak, canoe, or small boat with no gunwale to clip things to, a floating grip is worth the slight quality trade-off. Finding a grip on the bottom of a lake is an expensive afternoon.

**Jaw geometry.** Wider jaws give you more surface area on the fish's lip, which reduces the pressure per square inch and is better for the fish. Narrow jaw grips are lighter but they concentrate force — fine for bass, harder on species with more delicate mouths.

**Wrist lanyard attachment.** Every grip worth buying has a lanyard hole. Use it. A $30 grip goes overboard the same as a $10 one.

## How to Use a Fish Grip Correctly

A grip tool is only as good as the technique behind it. Here's what years of watching people misuse these things has taught us:

**Grip low on the jaw, not high.** The grip teeth should be on the lower lip only. Jamming the grip up into the fish's mouth increases injury risk and makes it harder to operate the hook remover with your other hand.

**Keep the fish vertical or close to it.** Fish grips let you hold a fish by the lip, which is designed for their jaw structure. What's not designed for their jaw structure is holding a large fish horizontally with all its body weight pulling on that single point. For fish over about 4 pounds, support the body with your other hand whenever possible.

**Don't swing fish.** For photos, support the fish or hold it over the water. Swinging fish for pictures by a lip grip is how you break jaws and release a fish that can't feed anymore.

**Release cleanly.** Once the hook is out and you're ready to release, get the fish in the water before you open the jaw. Let it swim out of the grip rather than dropping it.

## FAQ: Fish Grip Tools

**Q: Are fish grips bad for the fish?**
Used correctly, no — they're actually better for fish than bare-hand handling because they reduce the removal of slime coat and minimize handling time. The key is technique: grip the lower lip only, don't hold heavy fish horizontally by the lip for extended periods, and get the fish back in the water as quickly as possible. Used incorrectly — gripping too hard, holding large fish unsupported — they can cause jaw injuries.

**Q: Do I need a fish grip with a scale, or can I use a separate scale?**
Either works, but a combination grip-scale is genuinely more convenient in practice. When you're fishing alone, trying to hold a fish with one hand, operate a separate scale with the other, and not drop anything is an exercise in frustration. The built-in scale adds a few dollars to the price and a fraction of an ounce to the weight — worth it for most anglers.

**Q: What fish grip is best for saltwater?**
Any grip you plan to use in saltwater should be either stainless steel or marine-grade polymer. The Berkley Stainless and KastKing Stainless are the best picks from this list. Aluminum will hold up in saltwater if you're diligent about rinsing it after every use, but stainless requires significantly less maintenance.

**Q: Can I use a fish grip on any species?**
Fish grips work best on species that can be safely lip-gripped: bass, walleye, catfish, pike, musky, many inshore saltwater species. They're not appropriate for all fish — species with extremely delicate mouths, fish with teeth that extend to the lower jaw in a way that makes lip-gripping impossible, or fish you're planning to keep (where a scale bag is more practical) don't suit lip grips well. Always check whether lip-gripping is appropriate for the specific species you're targeting.

**Q: What size fish grip do I need?**
Longer grips (9"+) give you more leverage on bigger fish and keep your hands further from teeth and hooks. Shorter grips are lighter and more convenient for panfish and smaller species. If you're buying one grip that has to do everything, a 9-inch model like the Rapala is the right call. If you're specifically targeting large toothy predators, go to 9.5 inches or longer.

**Q: Are floating fish grips worth the trade-off in quality?**
Floating grips are made of buoyant plastic, which means they can't match the build quality of metal grips at the same price point. The trade-off is worth it for kayak anglers and anyone fishing situations where dropped gear is likely. If you're fishing from a stable boat with solid footing, a non-floating metal grip is the better tool.
