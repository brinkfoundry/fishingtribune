## What to Look For in a Fishing Tackle Bag

Before you buy anything, think through these key factors:

**Tray size compatibility.** Most tackle bags are designed around Plano's 3600 or 3700 series boxes. The 3700 is larger (14" x 9" x 1.75") and better for bigger lures — crankbaits, swimbaits, larger spoons. The 3600 is more compact (11" x 7" x 1.75") and works well for finesse plastics, jigs, and hardware. Know which system your existing tackle is organized in before buying a bag that fits the wrong size.

**Material and waterproofing.** There's a meaningful difference between water-resistant and waterproof. Water-resistant bags handle splash and light rain. Waterproof bags (like the Fishpond, which uses TPU laminate) handle submersion. Most anglers are fine with water-resistant unless you're wade fishing or kayak fishing where immersion is possible.

**Strap and carry system.** A loaded tackle bag is heavy. Six 3700 trays full of lures can weigh 15+ pounds. If you're walking any distance, padded straps matter. Boat anglers who just move the bag from truck to boat can live with minimal padding. Hikers and wade anglers need proper shoulder support.

**Organization beyond trays.** Trays handle your main lures, but you also need spots for pliers, line, hooks, sinkers, snaps, swivels, scissors, and terminal tackle. Bags that only think about tray storage make you stuff everything else in your pockets. Look for dedicated side pockets and an interior organizer panel.

**Durability at stress points.** Where bags fail: zipper pulls, handle stitching, strap attachment points, and zipper coils. Check these when evaluating any bag. Rubberized zipper pulls are more durable than cord pulls. Double-stitched handles outlast single-stitched handles. YKK zippers outlast generic zippers. These details separate bags that last five years from bags that last one season.
