## What to Look for in a Fishing Headlamp

**Lumens:** 200-400 is the practical sweet spot for most fishing tasks. Higher lumens are useful for navigation and open-water scanning but drain batteries faster. Don't chase maximum lumens unless you have a specific reason.

**Red Light Mode:** Non-negotiable for night fishing. Red preserves your night vision after the light is off, and it's significantly less likely to scatter fish feeding near the surface. A white light pointed at the water in low light conditions can shut down a bite in seconds.

**Waterproofing:** IPX4 handles rain and splash — fine for most boat and bank fishing. IPX6 and above handles sustained water exposure and is worth it for wade fishing and kayak anglers. IPX8 is submersion-rated and worth the premium if you're in or around water constantly.

**Battery System:** Rechargeable is convenient and cost-effective. Alkaline backup is a safety net on long trips. Dual-system lights (like the Actik Core and HL7R) offer the best of both.

**Weight and Fit:** A headlamp that slides around or creates pressure points after two hours is useless on a long session. Look for wide, padded headbands and secondary top straps if you're going to wear it for extended periods.

**Beam Pattern:** Flood beams illuminate a wide area — great for rigging, deck work, and camp use. Spot beams throw distance — useful for navigation and scanning. Mixed beams do both adequately. Know which scenario you're buying for.

## Red Light Mode: Why It Matters More Than You Think

This deserves its own section because it's the most fishing-specific feature on a headlamp and the one most general-purpose reviews undervalue.

Fish in low-light conditions are feeding actively and are keyed in on surface disturbance and light changes. A white headlamp sweeping the water surface — even briefly — can end a bite that took hours to develop. Red light is less visible to most freshwater and saltwater species because fish have different cone sensitivity than humans. It won't guarantee you don't spook fish, but it meaningfully reduces the risk.

For the angler: red light at low intensity preserves your rod-adapted night vision. When you switch the headlamp off, your eyes need 20-30 minutes to fully readjust to darkness. Red light at low output speeds that recovery significantly, meaning you can switch from rigging to watching your line without losing visibility.

Bottom line: if you're buying a headlamp specifically for night fishing, a functional red light mode is not optional.
