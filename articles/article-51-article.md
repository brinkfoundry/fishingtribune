## What Makes a Rod Good for Pier Fishing

Before we get into picks, it's worth being clear about what pier fishing actually demands — because it's different from shore fishing, boat fishing, or kayak fishing.

**Length matters more than you think.** On a pier, you're elevated above the water — anywhere from 8 feet to 30+ feet depending on the structure. A longer rod helps you cast farther out from the pier's shadow (where fish school), manage line angle when fighting fish, and maneuver around other anglers' lines. Most pier regulars fish 9- to 11-foot rods. Shorter rods (7'–8') are fine for drop rigs straight down or live bait fishing directly under the pier.

**Power and action for the species.** Pier fishing is mostly medium-heavy work. You're throwing 2–4 oz of weight plus bait, and targeting species from pompano and flounder to cobia and big redfish. Go too light and you can't cast the rigs; go too heavy and you'll miss bites.

**Guides and blanks that handle salt.** Corrosion is real. Cheap aluminum oxide guides pit out in a season on a saltwater pier. Look for stainless steel frames, aluminum oxide or titanium inserts, and blanks with proper saltwater-resistant finishes.

**Handle length.** Two-handed casting requires a longer butt section. Most pier rods run 10–14 inches of rear grip. Don't overlook this — casting a 4 oz rig single-handed is how people throw their backs out.
