Why Your Net Matters More Than You Think

Most anglers treat the net like an afterthought — something they grabbed off a peg hook at the gas station years ago. Then they lose a fish they'd been working for twenty minutes and spend the drive home blaming the hook gap.

It wasn't the hook gap.

A bad net kills fish, loses fish, and tangles hooks into a nightmare you're still picking apart when the bite turns back on. The right net lands fish cleanly, keeps them alive for a quick photo if you're releasing, and doesn't cost you a jig head every time you pull it out of the water.

There are three things that actually matter when you're picking a net:

Mesh type. Rubber mesh is gentler on fish — it doesn't strip slime coat, doesn't snag hooks, and doesn't tangle like a nightmare. Nylon is cheaper and fine for fish you're keeping. Knotless nylon is the middle ground. If you're catch-and-release at all, go rubber.

Hoop size. Bigger fish need bigger hoops. A 12-inch hoop is fine for panfish. A 36-inch hoop is what you want when you're netting a 50-inch muskie. Matching hoop size to target species is the single most common mistake I see on the water.

Handle length. Kayak anglers want short. Shore anglers need long. Boat anglers usually want something in the 24–36 inch range. This isn't complicated, but it matters.

Rubber vs. Nylon Mesh: The Real Answer

Rubber wins for catch-and-release. It doesn't remove slime coat, doesn't tangle hooks, and is faster to clean. The only downsides are cost and durability — rubber mesh can tear on sharp objects over time.

Knotless nylon is the choice for fish you're keeping, tournament scenarios where durability matters, and any situation where you're netting heavy fish repeatedly. It's not as gentle, but it lasts longer in hard use.

Knotted nylon mesh is what you find on cheap nets at gas stations. Avoid it. It tangles hooks, it's rough on fish, and the knots create failure points. There's no scenario where it's the right choice over knotless.

Floating vs. Non-Floating

Simple rule: if you wade or fish from a kayak, get a floating net. If you lose it, you want it on the surface. If you fish from a boat with rod holders and hook storage everywhere, floating matters less because you have somewhere to secure the net.

The EGO S2 is worth every penny of its premium if you wade. For boat anglers, it's a nice feature that probably won't save your life, and you can spend that money elsewhere.

FAQ

Q: What size net do I need for bass fishing?

A: For bass up to 5 pounds, a 18-20 inch hoop handles the job. If you're targeting fish over 5 pounds or fishing tournaments where you don't want any hesitation at the boat, go to a 24-inch hoop minimum. The Ranger Tournament Series 24x30 is built for exactly this.

Q: Is rubber mesh really better than nylon?

A: For catch-and-release, yes — meaningfully better. Rubber doesn't strip slime coat, which is the fish's primary defense against infection. It also doesn't tangle treble hooks, which saves you real time during a bite. If you're keeping fish, the advantage is smaller, but rubber still wins on the hook-tangle issue.

Q: Can I use a regular fishing net for ice fishing?

A: Not comfortably. Standard open-water nets have hoops too large to maneuver through a 10-inch hole, and handles too long for close-range control while kneeling. A dedicated ice fishing net like the Plano Frost costs $30 and solves both problems.

Q: How do I keep my net from tangling hooks during fishing?

A: First, switch to rubber mesh — it's dramatically less prone to hook tangles than any nylon option. Second, store the net with the hoop facing down so hooks naturally fall away from the mesh rather than into it. Third, when possible, remove hooks before netting — if you're using barbless hooks or can get pliers on before the net, do it.
