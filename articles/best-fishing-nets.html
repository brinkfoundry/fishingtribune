Why Your Net Actually Matters

Most anglers spend serious money on rods, reels, and line — then grab whatever net was hanging by the door. That's backward thinking.

A nylon mesh net with rough knotted strands strips slime coat and scales off fish you plan to release. Studies on catch-and-release survival rates consistently show that the landing method — including net type — affects post-release mortality. Rubber mesh eliminates most of that damage. A net with the wrong hoop size turns a simple scoop into a wrestling match. A handle that's six inches too short puts your face over the water at the worst possible moment.

The right net for your situation depends on three things: species and average fish size, whether you're in a boat or wading, and whether you're keeping or releasing.

Rubber vs. Nylon Mesh: What You Actually Need to Know

Rubber mesh is the right choice for any fish you plan to release. The smooth surface doesn't strip slime coat, doesn't remove scales, and doesn't leave fish struggling to breathe after you bring them aboard. Research on rainbow trout post-release survival consistently shows rubber-netted fish do better. The downside: rubber costs more, can stiffen in very cold water, and adds some weight.

Nylon mesh (knotless) is fine for fish you're keeping or for situations where cost is the primary concern. Knotless is meaningfully better than knotted — the rounded strand material causes less physical damage than rough-cut knotted nylon. But it's still nylon, and compared to rubber, fish take more damage.

The rule: If you practice catch-and-release regularly, pay for rubber. If you're keeping a limit of crappie, knotless nylon is fine.

Floating vs. Non-Floating Nets

A floating net sounds like a luxury feature until you drop your net off the side of a kayak or lose your grip while wading. Floating nets are made with foam-filled handles or foam blocks integrated into the frame. They won't sink to the bottom of a river or lake.

For wading: floating is essentially a requirement. For boat fishing: strongly recommended. For kayak fishing where the net is always clipped to the boat: less critical, but still nice to have.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What size landing net do I need for bass fishing?

For largemouth and smallmouth bass, a hoop size of at least 18 inches wide and 20 inches long is the practical minimum. A 20x23 hoop like the Frabill Conservation Series handles most bass you'll encounter from shore or a boat without difficulty. If you're fishing tournaments or trophy waters where you regularly catch fish over 5 pounds, go bigger.

Q: Is rubber mesh really worth the extra cost?

Yes, if you release fish. Rubber mesh causes significantly less physical damage than nylon — less scale loss, less slime coat stripping, less fin damage. Post-release survival rates are measurably better for rubber-netted fish. If you keep everything you catch, nylon knotless mesh is adequate and saves you $20–$50.

Q: Do I need a telescoping handle or is a fixed handle fine?

Depends entirely on how you fish. Boat anglers benefit from telescoping handles — you can adjust reach based on where fish are relative to the boat. Waders and kayak anglers often prefer fixed handles because they're lighter, sturdier, and there's nothing to adjust while you're managing a fish and your footing at the same time. Fixed handles also have fewer failure points.

Q: How do I keep a fish calm in the net while I remove the hook?

Keep the fish in the water in the net as much as possible. Submerged in the net bag, fish stay calmer than they do in open air. Use forceps or a dehooking tool to remove the hook quickly rather than fumbling with fingers. If you need a photo, lift the fish briefly, shoot the photo, and get it back in the water immediately. The net is a tool for landing the fish — not a holding pen.
