Why Your Net Actually Matters

Most anglers spend $300 on a rod and $12 on a net. Then they wonder why a 24-inch walleye did a backflip off the rim at the boat. The net is the last piece of equipment between you and a successful catch — or a story you'll tell for the wrong reasons.

The big choices that actually matter:

Rubber vs. Nylon mesh. Rubber mesh is heavier and costs more, but it doesn't snag hooks, doesn't strip fish slime (which matters for catch-and-release), and dries faster. Nylon is cheaper, lighter, and fine for harvest fishing. If you're releasing anything, get rubber.

Floating vs. non-floating. Kayak anglers and waders need floating nets — drop it and it sits on the surface instead of sinking to the bottom. Most boat anglers don't care. If you're on foot or in a kayak, floating is worth the extra few dollars.

Handle length. Wade fishing and bank fishing need longer handles — 4 to 6 feet. Boat fishing with low gunwales works fine with 24 to 36 inches. Don't buy a short handle for deep-sided vessels or you'll be hanging over the rail every time.

Hoop size. Match the hoop to the fish you're targeting. A 20-inch hoop handles bass and walleye fine. Pike, muskie, and big salmon need 28 inches or more. A small hoop on a big fish isn't a net — it's a suggestion.

Rubber vs. Nylon Mesh: The Short Version

Choose rubber if: You're releasing fish. Rubber mesh doesn't pull slime, doesn't tangle hooks, and dries faster than nylon. It costs more and weighs more but it's worth it for serious anglers.

Choose nylon if: You're keeping fish, fishing on a budget, or targeting smaller species where a premium mesh net is unnecessary. Knotless nylon is the next best option if you're releasing fish but don't want to pay rubber mesh prices.

Knotted nylon — the stuff on the cheapest nets — is what you're trying to avoid for catch-and-release. Hooks get tangled, fish get scraped, and every second a fish spends in the net fighting a hook tangle is a second its survival odds are dropping.

Floating vs. Non-Floating Nets: Who Actually Needs It

Floating nets cost a bit more and are usually built from foam-core or hollow handles and lighter frames. You need one if:

  • You fish from a kayak or canoe
  • You wade fish in moving water
  • You fish from a float tube or pontoon boat
  • You fish solo and can't afford to chase a dropped net

If you're fishing from a stable boat with a rod holder and a partner, you probably don't need a floating net. The extra cost buys you insurance you may never need.

FAQs

Q: What size net do I need for bass fishing?

A: For largemouth and smallmouth bass in the 2–5 pound range, a hoop size of 18 to 22 inches handles the fish comfortably. If you're targeting tournament-class fish over 5 pounds, go 22 to 24 inches. The depth of the net bag matters too — a shallow bag lets a bass tail its way back out while you're getting the hook out.

Q: Do rubber mesh nets really make a difference for catch-and-release?

A: Yes. Studies on trout and salmon show measurably higher post-release survival rates with rubber or knotless mesh nets compared to knotted nylon. The slime coat on fish is a disease barrier — rough mesh removes it. Rubber mesh also keeps hooks from tangling, which means less time in the net and less handling stress. If you're releasing fish, it matters.

Q: How long should a fishing net handle be?

A: Wade fishing: 48–72 inches. Kayak fishing: 36–48 inches. Boat fishing from low gunwales: 24–36 inches. High-sided boats or standing while fishing: 36–48 inches. The principle is simple — you should be able to reach the water surface with the net without leaning dangerously over the rail or straining in the current.

Q: Can I use a landing net for surf fishing?

A: A standard freshwater net won't hold up to surf conditions — saltwater corrodes aluminum frames and standard mesh, the waves make scooping erratic, and larger surf species need purpose-built equipment. For surf fishing, a gaff or a dedicated surf landing net with corrosion-resistant materials and a longer handle is a better fit. If you must use a standard net, rinse it thoroughly after every saltwater use.
