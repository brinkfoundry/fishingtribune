What Makes a Landing Net Actually Good

Before the list, three things that separate a net worth owning from one that costs you fish:

Mesh material. Nylon is cheap, snags hooks constantly, and strips slime coat off fish you plan to release. Rubber mesh is harder on your wallet but easier on fish and your sanity. Coated nylon splits the difference — better than bare nylon, not quite rubber.

Float or sink? Rubber nets are heavy. Drop one off a kayak and it's gone. Floating nets (usually foam-core handles or lightweight construction) matter enormously if you're fishing from a small watercraft. On a drift boat or shoreline, it's a non-issue.

Hoop size and handle length. Match the net to the fish and the situation. A 12-inch trout net won't swallow a 28-inch walleye. A 6-foot handle is useless in tight creek brush. Get specific.

Rubber vs. Nylon Mesh: Which Do You Actually Need?

Rubber mesh is the answer for catch-and-release fishing, period. It doesn't strip protective slime coat, doesn't tangle treble hooks, dries faster, resists odor, and is easier to clean. The trade-off is weight and cost. If you're releasing fish, spend the extra $20 and get rubber.

Nylon mesh is fine for anglers who keep their catch. Lighter, cheaper, widely available, and entirely functional for its intended purpose. The problem is that most anglers who think they fish for keeps release more fish than they admit. Nylon is also rough on fish you might release — the scale damage and slime loss increase mortality.

Coated nylon is a workable middle ground — better than bare nylon for slime retention, won't tangle hooks as aggressively, and usually floats better. Not as clean as rubber, but a reasonable compromise for anglers fishing varied presentations.

Floating vs. Non-Floating: When It Matters

If you fish from a kayak, canoe, float tube, or any small watercraft where you're leaning over the side, a floating net is a practical necessity. Rubber-mesh nets in particular will sink fast — the mesh is dense, the frame is heavy, and a 5-foot drop puts it 10 feet deep before you react.

Shoreline and bank anglers can ignore this spec entirely. Boat anglers working with a net holder clipped to the gunwale have low drop risk. Wade fishers working moderate current should think about it — a telescoping net slipping off a wading belt is gone in moving water.

If you're on moving water or any situation where the net could leave your hand, floating matters.

FAQ

Q: Can I use a landing net for catch-and-release fishing?

Yes, but mesh type matters enormously. Nylon mesh removes slime coat and can cause scale damage that increases post-release mortality. Rubber mesh keeps the fish's protective coating intact, reduces hook tangling, and makes for faster releases. If you're releasing fish, rubber mesh isn't optional — it's the responsible choice. All five nets in this guide can be used for catch-and-release, but the nylon Beckman is the weakest choice for it.

Q: What size landing net do I need for bass fishing?

For largemouth and smallmouth bass, an 18×22-inch to 22×24-inch hoop covers the vast majority of fish you'll encounter. The Frabill Conservation Series at 20×23 inches is the right size for bass up to the 5–6 lb range. If you're consistently fishing trophy fisheries where 8+ lb bass are realistic, size up to the EGO S2's 22×24-inch hoop and 24-inch bag depth. The bag depth matters as much as the hoop — a big bass can flip out of a shallow bag before you get control.

Q: Should I get a telescoping handle or fixed handle?

Depends entirely on how you fish. Fixed handles are stiffer, simpler, and don't fail under load — the right choice for musky and pike fishing where the forces involved are serious. Telescoping handles give you reach adjustment, which matters for wade fishing and bank situations where you can't always get directly above the fish. The EGO S2's telescoping mechanism is the best available at the non-commercial level. For general bass and walleye fishing, telescoping is the more versatile option.
