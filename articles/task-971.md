## How to Choose: What Actually Matters

**Polarization is non-negotiable.** Every pair on this list is polarized. If you're looking at fishing sunglasses that aren't polarized, keep walking. Polarization is what eliminates horizontal light waves reflecting off the water surface — without it, you're just wearing tinted glasses and doing nothing for your ability to see fish.

**Glass vs. Polycarbonate:** Glass lenses are optically superior — sharper, better color rendering, more scratch-resistant. They're also heavier and will crack if dropped. Polycarbonate is lighter, impact-resistant, and won't shatter, but it scratches more easily and has slightly lower optical clarity. For the clearest sight fishing in the best conditions, go glass. For rough use and active fishing, polycarbonate is practical.

**Lens tint by condition:**
- **Copper/Brown:** Best all-around for variable conditions, excellent contrast in green and blue water
- **Gray:** Best for bright offshore days, most accurate color rendering
- **Yellow/Amber:** Low light, overcast, dawn and dusk fishing
- **Blue Mirror:** Bright sun, shallow tropical water
- **Green Mirror:** General purpose, excellent in moderate light

**Frame fit and coverage:** More coverage equals more protection from side glare and UV. Wraparound frames are better for active fishing. Make sure the frame sits close to your face — gaps on the sides let in peripheral light that kills contrast.

**UV protection:** Every pair listed here provides 100% UVA/UVB protection. Don't buy any pair that doesn't. Prolonged UV exposure without proper eye protection causes cataracts and macular degeneration. This isn't marketing — it's the reason fishing guides have eye surgery at higher rates than the general population.
